Every few months someone publishes a "how to stay relevant in the AI age" article, and every few months it says the same thing: learn to prompt better. I've read enough of them.

But something Ajit Jaokar wrote about Claude Skills made me stop and actually think. His argument flips the usual framing entirely.

## It's not about using AI. It's about teaching it.

Claude Skills are reusable instruction packages — plain-English `.md` files that tell Claude how to handle a specific type of task. When the right context appears, Claude loads the Skill automatically. No code. No APIs. Just structured documentation.

What's interesting isn't the technology. It's what it implies about expertise.

Right now, most "AI reskilling" programs teach people how to talk to AI. Write better prompts. Get better outputs. That's useful, but it's shallow. The deeper skill — and the one that actually creates career leverage — is knowing your own workflows clearly enough to write them down for someone else.

That someone else just happens to be an AI.

## Who actually has an edge here?

Not the generalist AI practitioner. The nurse who knows exactly how triage decisions get made. The paralegal who could write the intake checklist in their sleep. The compliance officer who's memorized the exception rules everyone else looks up.

Those people have tacit knowledge — the kind that lives in their heads and never makes it into documentation. Claude Skills are a vehicle for extracting it. And because the format is plain English, there's no developer required to act as translator.

The reskilling opportunity isn't "learn AI." It's "understand your own work well enough to encode it."

## The tension worth naming

There's an obvious discomfort here. If your expertise gets encoded into a Skill, does that diminish your value?

Maybe — if your value was purely in execution. But the professionals who will thrive are the ones who move up a level: governing the Skills they built, refining them when edge cases break them, improving them as the work evolves. The tacit knowledge doesn't disappear. It just moves from being locked in one person's head to being accessible at scale.

## Skills as credentials

One framing I keep coming back to: Skills as portable professional credentials. If you've built a well-tested, well-documented Skill that captures a real domain process, that's demonstrable expertise in a form that travels with you.

That's a more interesting resume artifact than "proficient with ChatGPT."

The question worth sitting with: what workflow do you know so well that you could write the instructions from memory? That's probably where your first Skill should start.
